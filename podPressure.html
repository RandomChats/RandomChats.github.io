Pod Pressure (AR Game)

The Project

This was a group project that I was a part of for my GIMM110 course. The goal of this project was to make a game that incorporated AR elements into a 2D game that we were to work on. We decided after talking amongst ourselves to make a 2D platformer game that would use AR Elements in mini-games, taking elements from games like Keep Talking and Nobody Explodes. I was one of the level designers and the AR developer for this project, using the experience I've had previously working with AR, I assumed initially that this project would be fairly straightforward, but that was far from the case. When working with the AR elements, and the in-game elements, I used Unity and their XR Toolkit.

What I worked through

Being upfront with this project, at least in my eyes, it was overall a failure. I remember many times in the early stages of our project, we had to change our ideas time and time again because we kept finding that it was either too ambitious for our group, we wouldn't have enough time, or our professor pointed out crucial flaws about the gameplay loop. After eventually landing on the game idea we all had, it already felt like we were slightly behind on our group work. This didn't stop all of us, however, and we began working on the game when we all came to a consensus. The first aspect of the game we worked through was the basic 2D game itself, this didn't take much time, and we were able to very quickly get the level and side-level fields made up. The issue comes from the AR itself. Our idea was that there would be a map that would be displayed on an outward-facing camera, and a second player would be assisted through the maze from the first player. A very large wall that I encountered time and time again was the collision between the player moving through the maze. I could get the map to display on the screen, and I could get the player icon to show up on the screen, but no matter what I tried, I was unable to figure out how to get the collisions to work. I spent hours researching the problem and got assistance from multiple people who had worked with AR in the past, but nothing seemed to work out perfectly. As the deadline for the project neared, I realized what the issue was, and learned that the issue was irreversible. When I was generating the second player's game object to move through the maze, what I failed to realize was that the maze I had embedded on the screen would also move with the game object, but would still display properly. This meant it also moved the colliders for the maze, making it near impossible to get the objects separated. I tried to figure out a solution to this, but as the deadline came, I was unable to get a fix working for the game.

What I Learned

Overall, this project was very stressful for me, but in this stress, I was able to learn that even if I'm facing a challenge, having the determination to push forward and work through the issues facing me is extremely beneficial to me. While I feel I was unable to meet the objectives I set out for myself, I also learned to properly lower my scope and to properly pivot when needed. If I decided earlier on to switch to a different concept for our AR usage, it would have been great for us to make that adjustment. This project also taught me that if something isn't working for me, I need to change my ideas or get other opinions to see if my work would be too far-fetched.
